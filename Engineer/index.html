Current Position

J.F.Shea Construction
Structural Concrete Estimator
(2012 - present)

Description

Since January 2012 I have been working as a structural concrete estimator. I have been responsible for concrete quantity take-offs and pricing for very diverse water infrastructure projects, from construction of new facilities, to rehabilitation of existing structures or seismic retrofits.

The Best Parts

Thanks to my position as a structural estimator I have had the chance to learn a completely new discipline. As explained below, my field experience was highly focused on mechanical, commissioning and start-up. After I finished the Headworks project and came into our office to help estimate, I knew very little about concrete construction or formwork. However, we had enough mechanical estimators at the time, and needed some help with concrete, so, with the assistance of our senior structural estimator, I jumped right in. The second project I was asked to estimate was a $200M water treatment facility, so I had to learn fast!

When you are in the field, you are involved with a single project for a long period of time, and you get to know that project in great detail. But it takes a long time until you move on to a different job. As an estimator, you only spend a few days or weeks working on any particular job. You get exposed to many more jobs, and I do enjoy this aspect of my position. Although the downside is that you know them in a more superficial manner. This is especially true for the jobs for which we do not become the successful low bidder.

Probably the most important part of my job as an estimator is tracking the performance of jobs that we do end up building. This is what keeps me honest, and tells me if the assumptions I made were correct, if I understood the job correctly, if the production rates used were accurate, etc. Following closely the jobs I estimated has been a critical aspect of my work in estimating.

One of the most appealing aspects of my position is the "every job is a puzzle" aspect of it. Every project we estimate has peculiarities that makes it unique. I enjoy figuring out where the difficulty lies for each one of them, understanding what the schedule and the work constraints are going to be like, and how they will affect cost.

Finally, and this is also important, I get to work heavily with Excel. Yes, I love Excel. I love writing crazy, beautiful, multiple line formulas. It's the closest I get to programming at work. I have heavily refactored our take-off workbook, and this has helped me automate many tasks that previously had to be done manually. The result is more accurate and detailed estimates for less work. I have also built a spreadsheet system, ranging multiple workbooks, that allows me to track job performance weekly across multiple projects. Yes, we did not really have such a system before. And yes, perhaps we should be using a database instead.

The Challenges

The first, most obvious challenge, has been estimating a new discipline without direct field experience in it (and overcoming the impostor syndrome associated with it). The answer, I think, has been to ask more experienced people lots of questions, especially dumb ones. We have a few excellent concrete superintendents and foremen and, whenever I see them, I try to bombard them with questions as much as I can (sorry, guys). It also helps me to call them or visit them when I have a job that seems particularly challenging.

A critical weapon to fight off the impostor syndrome problem has been, as explained above, to closely track job performance, especially the jobs I have estimated. That's been a humbling and extremely valuable experience. I have also been involved with some jobs during the submittal and planning phases.

I have learned that drawing structures, and even preparing preliminary key plans showing the different concrete placements, as well as formwork sketches, can be tremendously valuable during the estimating phase. Drawing 3D models of complex structures has also helped me at times.

When I first started working as an estimator, I had to become productive very quickly because we had a lot of work to bid with tight deadlines. The result was that I had to use many general rules of thumb and assumptions. Part of my work since then has been to unpack those rules of thumb, learn what is needed for each particular situation, and be more detailed-oriented.

The biggest challenge of all, an industry-wide one, is having to compete on price only. There is no brand, no user experience, no customer relations in public work bidding. It's all about being the lowest bidder. This can be frustrating. I secretly envy other industries where you don't have to race to the bottom, and can instead strive to create the best product and provide a great experience.

Previous Jobs

Mejoras Energéticas (Madrid, Spain)
Consultant Engineer
2001 - 2003

Description

My first job out of college consisted of building computer models of water supply, sewer and storm drain systems. We used these models to simulate extreme loading conditions and future city expansion plans, find areas where existing infrastructure needed improvement and propose solutions.

The Best Parts

Having direct contact with customers is always a useful learning experience. The same can be said for managing a group of people. I extended my knowledge of hydraulics and hydrology, and, on the software side, learned to use GIS software MapInfo pretty well, got some exposure to relational databases and wrote basic SQL queries.

The Challenges

Most Spanish municipalities at the time did not have at their disposal all the information that the InfoWorks models require. In order to complete the work, we had to make assumptions, and no matter how reasonable we thought our assumptions were, one has to question the accuracy and usefulness of the model and, therefore, our whole endeavor. In other words, I am not sure that we brought a lot of value to our customers, and that was in my view the biggest challenge.
